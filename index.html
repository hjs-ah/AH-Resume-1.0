Chess Not Checkers — marriage, leadership & purpose panel

There's something that happens when you put a group of men in a room and ask them to be honest — not performatively honest, but genuinely, uncomfortably, productively honest. That's what we built space for at this year's Chess Not Checkers panel on marriage, leadership, and purpose.

We had married men and unmarried men in the same room. I've done panels before where you separate them, keep the conversations clean and compartmentalized. This time we didn't. And what came out of that was something I didn't fully anticipate — not conflict, but resonance. The married men weren't speaking down to the unmarried ones. The unmarried men weren't envying or dismissing. They were listening to each other.

The question that stayed with me most came from one of our younger guys: "How do you build something lasting when everything around you is telling you to keep your options open?" We spent 20 minutes on that one question. We didn't fully answer it. But we sat in it together, and that mattered.

This is what Chess Not Checkers is for. Not just to deliver programming, but to create rooms where men can think out loud about the things that actually shape their lives.

On Sabbath, rest, and the discipline of stopping

I've kept Sabbath — Friday evening through Saturday — as a non-negotiable protected period for several years now. Most people in my professional world don't know this. It doesn't come up in pipeline reviews.

What I've found is that Sabbath doesn't subtract from productivity. It restructures my relationship to it. When you know that Friday at sundown everything stops — the emails, the builds, the planning docs — you become sharper about what actually needs to happen before then. You make decisions faster. You let go of things that were never really yours to carry.

The deeper thing, though, is what Sabbath does to your sense of time. The week has a shape. It has a rhythm. There's a beginning and an end, and the end isn't Sunday night dread — it's rest. Real rest. Not scrolling rest. Not half-watching a show rest. The kind of rest that you have to choose and then protect.

I don't write about faith much in professional contexts. But I think about what I practice and what I produce, and they are not unrelated. The discipline of stopping is the same discipline that makes it possible to start well.
